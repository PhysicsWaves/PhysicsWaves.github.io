Resonance

Basics of Resonance

In sound applications, a resonant frequency is a natural frequency of vibration determined by the physical parameters of the vibrating object. This same basic idea of physically determined natural frequencies applies throughout physics in mechanics, electricity and magnetism, and even throughout the realm of modern physics. Some of the implications of resonant frequencies are:it is easy to get an object to vibrate at its resonant frequencies, hard to get it to vibrate at other frequencies, a vibrating object will pick out its resonant frequencies from a complex excitation and vibrate at those frequencies, essentially "filtering out" other frequencies present in the excitation, most vibrating objects have multiple resonant frequencies. Examples of this are springs, twin tuning forks, and whirling Tubes

Tuning Forks

Basics of Resonance

A tuning fork is used as a reference pitch to tune an instrument. They can be used for any instrument. String players will usually have an A 440 fork. Piano tuners will typically have either a C fork or an F fork. You sometimes see timpani players use them when they have to retune in the middle of a piece. One slight problem with tuning forks is that they can go slightly sharp or flat depending on the temperature. Since we have electronic tuners today that are more stable and easier to use than forks most musicians have switched to those. The major exception is piano tuners who tune aurally will often prefer to use a fork to set their temperament.

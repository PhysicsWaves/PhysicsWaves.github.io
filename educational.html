Doppler Effect

Basics of The Doppler Effect

The doppler effect is an increase (or decrease) in the frequency of sound, light, or other waves as the source and observer move toward (or away from) each other. The effect causes the sudden change in pitch noticeable in a passing siren, as well as the redshift seen by astronomers. When a sound is moving toward you, the waves would travel at a shorter distance, in less time, and you would hear a higher frequency. Sonic Boom

A sonic boom occurs as the result of the piling up of compressional wavefronts along the edge of the wave. These compressional wavefronts pile up and interfere to produce a very high-pressure zone. Instead of these compressional regions reaching you one at a time, they all reach you at once. Since every compression is followed by a rarefaction, the high-pressure zone will be immediately followed by a low-pressure zone. This creates a very loud noise. If you are standing on the ground as the supersonic aircraft passes by, there will be a short time delay and then you will hear the boom. This boom is just a loud noise from the high pressure sound followed by a low pressure sound. Sonic booms are observed when any aircraft that is traveling faster than the speed of sound passes overhead.
